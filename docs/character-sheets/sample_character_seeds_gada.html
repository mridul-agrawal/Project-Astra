Project Astra · Character Design Reference · Vol. 05

Four Wielders of the Gadā

All male, all mace-fighters — but four entirely different shapes the iron club takes when held by very different men. A young heir, an old wanderer, a priest who refused his birthright, and a freed pit-fighter.

Why four mace-fighters look this different

The gadā branch of the weapon tree is usually summarised as "brute force, low astra ceiling" — the body's weapon, not the mantra's. That summary makes mace-wielders look interchangeable. They aren't. The mace can be a coronation gift, a hidden walking-staff, a chosen alternative to scripture, or a piece of one's own former chains re-forged. Each origin produces a wholly different combatant, with wholly different tactics and wholly different silhouettes.

The four below share weapon, gender, and discipline. They share almost nothing else.

XVII
Seed · 17 — The Mountain-Prince

Nirjhara, Heir of Hemavata

A young upland prince whose coronation gift was a single piece of meteoric iron, and who would prefer, on most days, to be reading in his tent.

⤿ Gadayudha 01Royalty 02Maharathi · T5 03Mace 04Hill-Elephant 05Astra · Tier B 06Institutional Vow 07Pure Mortal 08Institutional School 09Dharma-Bound
Concept

Nirjhara is the twenty-three-year-old crown prince of Hemavata — a small upland kingdom that holds three high passes between the lowlands and the great snow-range to the north. The kingdom is small, prosperous, and cold; its court is austere, its prayers are short, and its warriors are taught early that a mace is a tool first and a weapon second. Stones fall on hill-roads. Boulders close passes. The mace was the lever that moved them. The same hands that moved boulders, when needed, defended the kingdom.

Nirjhara's coronation gift, given to him on his sixteenth birthday in the old custom, was not jewellery. It was a single mace forged from a piece of meteoric iron that had fallen on the kingdom's tallest peak in his great-grandfather's reign. The fragment had been kept in the temple's high gallery for ninety years; the smiths had argued for thirty years over how to forge it. They settled it the day Nirjhara was born. It is one piece of dark iron, with the chondritic flecks of its origin still visible at oblique angles — small bright points scattered across the head, like the night sky pressed flat. It is the only mace of its kind, in the kingdoms or out.

The trouble is that Nirjhara would rather be reading. He inherits a court that expects a war-prince and finds, instead, a serious young man who memorises long passages of philosophical commentary and goes quiet at dinner when asked about the borders. He is not a coward; he is, by every honest measure, lethal with the mace and unflinching on the field. He simply does not enjoy it. The campaign meets him at the moment his court has run out of patience for the distinction.

Appearance

Twenty-three; tall but not yet fully into his shoulders — the body of a young man whose training is almost complete but who has not, yet, lived in his own height. Skin a warm fair-tan, lighter than lowland nobility, slightly wind-burned at the cheekbones. Hair black with a faint copper undertone in sunlight, kept just past the shoulder, tied at the nape with a length of plain dark cord; a few unruly strands at the temples that he absent-mindedly tucks behind his ears mid-conversation.

Face: long, slightly aquiline; high cheekbones; a small studious mouth. The face of someone who reads a lot and is not, in temperament, a hunter. Eyes a clear cold-grey — the upland grey of mid-winter sky over snow — flecked, in the left iris only, with a single warm gold fleck. Court astrologers of Hemavata read the fleck as a sign of his line; he privately suspects it is a freckle.

Wears the formal kit of the Hemavata court: a layered armour of overlapping iron-blue lacquer plates over a high-collared dark-grey tunic, sleeves to the wrist for the cold, fur-trimmed at the cuffs. A long pale-grey traveling cloak with the kingdom's sigil — a six-pointed star formed of crossed mace-shafts — embroidered in dull silver at the shoulder. Heavy riding-boots in dark leather. A single silver brow-band at his hairline, plain, no jewels.

The gadā, named Tārā-Loṣṭa ("Star-Stone"), is in the high classical Hemavata court form — emphatically not a western-style hand-mace. Stood butt-down on the floor, the crown of the bulb rises a full hand-span above Nirjhara's head; the whole weapon is taller than he is. The haft is a single thick column of seasoned mountain-pine darkened to near-black with mineral oil, wide enough at the grip to require a double-handed clasp, banded with heavy plain iron at four points — pommel, lower grip, mid-haft, and bulb-collar.

The head is the entire weapon. A vast onion-bulb of meteoric iron roughly the size of Nirjhara's own chest sits at the crown, deeply fluted into eight broad vertical lobes that swell outward at the bulb's middle and taper at top and bottom in the deep classical lotus-bud profile. Two wide raised iron bands circle the bulb horizontally — one at the lower third, one at the upper third — both engraved in low relief with the silhouettes of the high passes of Hemavata. At the bulb's apex sits a stylised silver lotus-petal cap, eight petals matching the eight lobes, with a small tapered iron finial at its centre. Where bulb meets haft, an inverted silver lotus-petal collar wraps the seam, eight petals folded downward over the iron band beneath. The chondritic specks of the meteoric iron scatter across all eight lobes; in oblique light the whole bulb looks faintly star-mapped. Around the lower band, a single fine engraved ring bears four names: great-grandfather, grandfather, father, Nirjhara.

The weight is consequential. Nirjhara is one of perhaps five men in his kingdom who can swing the gadā two-handed for sustained combat. He carries it grounded, butt to the dust, when not riding; on his hill-elephant he braces it upright in a tall iron sleeve set into the howdah's frame.

His mount is a hill-elephant — smaller and more agile than the great war-elephants of the lowland kingdoms, the size of a large horse, dark grey, named Hima-Mukha ("Snow-Faced"). The hill-elephant's footing is sure on rocky ground that would lame a horse. Most lowlanders have never seen one and read the silhouette wrong from a distance.

Personality

Quiet, considered, slightly self-doubting. Reads philosophy and the older commentaries on dharma in his tent at night; carries a small leather-bound notebook in which he writes single-line responses to passages he finds dishonest. Has a younger sister he loved very much who died of fever at age fourteen, and a quiet permanent grief about it that he does not bring up but does not hide. The grief has, in the way of grief, made him kinder; it has also made him ferocious in the field when he feels someone is being bullied who shouldn't be.

Treats his elephant well, his soldiers well, his enemies politely. Treats his court — the senior advisors who have wanted him to be a different prince — with stiff, immaculate courtesy. They notice. He is aware they notice.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Heavy front-line champion with mobility unusual for his weight class. The hill-elephant gives him better movement and turning radius than a war-elephant warrior, but lower elevation and lower trampling damage. He fights more like a heavy cavalry officer than a classical Gaja unit — he can flank, can break a line, can withdraw and re-engage.

Tier B astras — uncommon on a mace-fighter — are his court tutors' work; they are ice-and-snow elementals well suited to his line and his terrain. He can briefly freeze a tile of ground (movement-cost penalty) or cause a small avalanche on a sloped map (3-tile slide). His passive aura grants nearby allies +1 morale-saves and a small bonus to operations on cold or rocky maps.

Lore Fragment

The smiths of Hemavata argued for thirty years about what to do with the meteor. Some said a sword; some, a great chain to close the high pass; one, an inkstand for the temple library. The argument settled, in the end, by a calendar — the prince was born, the gift was needed, the iron's voice (the smiths said) had grown clear by then. The mace is a single piece because a mace can be a tool. The smiths' final note, kept in the temple registry, observes that an inkstand could not have been.— from the Hemavata smiths' guild log, ninetieth winter

XVIII
Seed · 18 — The Hidden Mace

Praśānta, The Walker With The Bound Staff

An old wandering mendicant whose walking-staff is iron under its cloth, and who has not, in twenty years, swung it without being asked.

⤿ Gadayudha (Concealed) 01Liminal Birth 02Atirathi · T4 03Mace 04Foot 05Astra · Tier A 06Vow of Non-Initiation 07Pure Mortal 08Self-Taught 09Strategic-Neutral
Concept

Praśānta was, twenty-five years ago, a junior priest at a small temple in a town the campaign will never visit, in a kingdom that has since changed its name. Something happened — the campaign learns the outline only late, and the details never — that caused him to leave his order, leave his town, and leave behind the priestly name he had used for thirty years. He took the road, took an iron mace whose origin he will not discuss, wrapped it in plain cloth so it looked like an ordinary walking-staff, and started walking.

He has been walking since. He has crossed seven kingdoms on foot in twenty years. He carries nothing else: a small bundle of necessities, the cloth-wrapped mace, a wooden begging-bowl, a single change of clothing. He does not stay anywhere longer than seven nights — a private rule of his own — and he does not, ever, accept money for the use of his strength. He accepts food, shelter, and conversation; not coin.

The vow that orders his combat is small and exact: he will not strike first. The wrapping on the staff is not disguise; it is structural. As long as no one threatens him or the people he is walking with, the staff stays a staff. The cloth comes off only in the moment the mace is needed, and goes back on the moment after. He has been seen, by reliable witnesses, to unwrap the staff between two strikes and then re-wrap it before the third — to give the man swinging at him a chance to think again. The man, on those occasions, has usually thought again.

The campaign meets him on a high road, walking the wrong direction at twilight, and he agrees to walk with the party for a while. Not to fight for them. To walk with them. The fighting, if it comes, will come.

Appearance

Mid-fifties, tall and lean — the long-walked leanness of a man who eats once a day and walks fifteen miles between meals. Skin a deep tobacco-brown, weathered to fine soft creases at the corners of the eyes and across the back of the neck. Hair fully grey, kept long and gathered into a single low knot at the nape; a long grey beard that he keeps roughly trimmed, no more. The hands of a man who has walked with a staff for twenty years: knuckles slightly enlarged from grip, calluses at the palm, the right hand's index and middle fingers very slightly bent.

Eyes a soft, watchful dark-brown — the eyes of someone who notices everything and comments on almost none of it. He has a way of looking at people for a beat longer than is comfortable, with great kindness, that makes it impossible to lie to him for the next two sentences.

Wears the road-mendicant's habit: a simple wrap-tunic in undyed hemp gone the colour of dust, a long ochre upper-cloth thrown over one shoulder, both stained and faded by long sun and long road. A simple hemp cord at the waist. Bare feet in summer; soft cloth wraps in winter. No jewellery, no caste-mark, no insignia. He looks, on first inspection, like every other wandering mendicant on every other road.

The staff is the entire trick — and the trick depends on its true form being a full classical gadā in the great working-priestly scale, not anything resembling a hand-mace. Underneath the wrapping is a weapon that, stood butt-down, reaches the crown of Praśānta's own head: a long heavy haft of black-stained mountain-wood, two-handed, banded in plain iron at four points, and at the crown a vast onion-bulb head of dark working iron, wider than his own torso, fluted into six broad vertical lobes that swell at the bulb's middle and narrow at top and bottom in the deep classical lotus-bud profile. Two plain raised iron rings circle the bulb horizontally — at the lower third and at the upper third — entirely undecorated. A simple flared iron collar where bulb meets haft; a plain unornamented iron pommel at the foot. The kind of unadorned, honest gadā a country temple's night-watch keeper might once have been issued and then quietly kept when the temple shut its gates for the season.

The wrappings are extraordinary by necessity, because the bulb's silhouette has to be hidden. Many layers of plain undyed cloth are wound from butt to crown; over the bulb itself, the cloth is layered most generously — pad upon pad upon pad — and pressed into a long taper, so that the swell of the head is buried in cloth volume and reads, from any angle past two paces, as a thick uneven hand-bound grip on the upper third of an ordinary walking-staff rather than a head at all. A long thin cord runs spirally up the whole length, securing the cloth and looking, intentionally, like a peasant's binding. The wrappings are old and slightly grey; he re-wraps them twice a year. The cloth doubles as friction surface, as protective layer, and as rate-limiter — he must unwrap it to use it, which is, by his vow, what he wants.

He looks like nothing on the road. People walk past him. When the cloth comes off — a single trained motion, the cord uncoiled in three seconds, the great onion-bulb rolling into view at full head-height — most of them have just walked past the wrong man. The reveal, by long testimony, is the single most disconcerting moment in any combat involving him: the staff that was a staff is now a weapon visibly heavier than its bearer should be able to swing.

Personality

Gentle, slow-spoken, preternaturally observant. Will sit with a wounded stranger for an entire afternoon without speaking, simply being present. Knows the names of trees and uses them. Has stories about every road he has walked, and tells them only if asked, only the parts the asker actually needs.

Carries a quiet permanent sadness. The party will not learn what it is, exactly; the player will, by hints, work out enough to know not to ask. He does not, ever, raise his voice. The one time the campaign sees him angry, in a single late chapter, the temperature in the scene appears to drop by several degrees and most NPCs in the immediate area find a reason to be elsewhere.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Pure counter-attacker on foot. Like Vol. 03's Vidyutsmitā, Praśānta has no Attack action on the player's turn — but the mechanism is different: the player can spend his action to unwrap the staff, which is a one-turn buff state. While the staff is unwrapped, every enemy attack against him or against an adjacent ally generates a free counter-strike from him with his full Atirathi-tier mace stats. After two turns of unwrapped state, he automatically re-wraps and the buff ends.

His Tier A astras are minimal — small elemental wards he learned in his old order, used here to protect, not to strike. The unwrap-counter-rewrap pattern is the entire tactical loop. Players who try to keep him permanently unwrapped find that he refuses on subsequent turns; the cloth goes back on by itself.

Signature Ability

Between Two Strikes

Madhya-Bandha

After felling an attacker, Praśānta re-wraps the cloth on the staff in a single fluid motion before striking again — and offers, in that pause, a single sentence to any remaining enemy in melee range: a question, an observation, a name. Enemies under a morale-fail threshold (Skill check) immediately stand down for the rest of the engagement. Those who pass continue, and he resumes counter-attacking. The pause is, mechanically, a one-turn ceasefire offer.

Costonce per battle; the turn it is used, Praśānta cannot move and cannot counter-attack — only speak. If no enemy stands down, the next enemy strike against him deals +50% damage (he has, briefly, given up his guard)

Gameplay Feel

"He never starts a fight. He has, at the time of writing, never lost one. The two facts are, by his own assessment, related."

Lore Fragment

The innkeeper at the Three-Door Caravanserai keeps a small ledger of guests who paid in something other than coin. The entry for the wandering man who comes through every other autumn reads: walked an old woman home through the storm; mended the roof of the south stable; said three words to the widow at the well. Bed and bowl, two nights, no charge. The entry has been there for nineteen years. The handwriting is the innkeeper's. The wandering man has never seen it.— ledger of the Three-Door Caravanserai, kept by the inn's elder daughter

XIX
Seed · 19 — The Iron-Tongued

Lohajihva, The Priest Who Chose The Body

A Brahmin-born warrior who refused his lineage's mantric specialty for the iron of the gadā, and who speaks only at sunrise and sunset.

⤿ Gadayudha 01Priestly-Born Warrior 02Maharathi · T5 03Mace 04Foot 05Astra · Tier B (Body-Channelled) 06Ascetic Vow (Partial Silence) 07Pure Mortal 08Institutional School 09Dharma-Bound
Concept

Lohajihva is the unusual case of a Brahmin-Warrior who, at his graduation from the order he was raised in, refused the mantric specialty his lineage had prepared him for and asked instead to be apprenticed in the iron club. The order considered it a regression. He considered it a refinement. The argument, to the senior teachers, was unanswerable: the mantra is an extension of the body. If the body is not first understood, the mantra is misused. I will spend a decade with the iron, and I will return to the mantra when I know what I am. He left for the smiths' quarter.

The decade became fifteen years, then twenty. He has not returned to mantric specialty. He has, instead, become a Maharathi-tier mace-fighter who channels the order's mantric training through the body — his Tier B astras are not ranged elementals but body-empowering recitations: short whispered mantras at the moment of impact that briefly increase the mace's effective mass, the body's stability, the strike's penetration. He fights as a kind of mantric-percussive instrument, and the order's senior teachers — when they are honest — admit that the result has been remarkable.

He took an additional vow on his thirtieth birthday: he speaks only at sunrise and at sunset. The remainder of the day, he communicates by gesture, by written tablet, by a small wax-board he keeps at his belt. The vow began as an experiment in restraint; he kept it because it improved his attention. The party meets him used to it. New acquaintances find it difficult; old companions find it efficient.

Appearance

Mid-thirties. Broad and powerfully built — the body of a man who has trained six hours a day with a heavy mace for twenty years and has eaten, throughout, exactly what his order prescribed. Skin a deep walnut-brown, weathered at the forearms and across the back of the neck, scarred lightly along the right shin from a training accident at twenty. He moves with an unhurried economy that some characters mistake for slowness; on the field, the motion is unmistakable.

Hair black, kept very short — almost cropped — shorter than priestly tradition prefers, a small visible badge of the choice he made. A close-trimmed black beard. Eyes a steady dark-brown, deeply set, very still. His face has the unusual quality of being almost expressionless in repose and quite warm when he smiles, which is sparing.

Wears the order's plain ochre robes adapted for combat: a long upper-cloth gathered and belted at the waist, a knee-length wrap below it, a wide grey-brown sash. Across his bare right shoulder runs the priestly thread of his order — the yajñopavīta — undyed and visible, the only mark that he is, technically, still of the order he is sometimes accused of having left. He carries a small wax-board on a thin leather cord at his belt for written speech, with a stylus tucked into the binding. He wears no jewellery. He goes barefoot in camp and in soft-leather wraps in the field.

The gadā, named Mauna-Loṣṭra ("Silent-Iron"), is the Vāgmuni Order's high-classical priestly form, in the great scale. Stood butt-down, the crown stands a full hand above Lohajihva's head — the whole weapon is taller than its bearer. The haft is a thick column of black-stained ironwood, two-handed in standard grip, banded in heavy iron at five points (pommel, lower grip, mid-haft, upper haft, and bulb-collar). At the foot is a heavy eight-petal iron lotus pommel, petals fully open; matching it at the crown of the bulb is a closed eight-petal lotus cap with a small inward-curving iron finial at its centre.

The bulb is the dominating element — a vast onion-form of plain dark working iron roughly the size of a temple-attendant's chest, deeply fluted into eight tall vertical lobes that swell outward at the bulb's middle in the deep priestly profile. Three wide raised iron bands circle the bulb horizontally — at the lower third, at the equator, and at the upper third — dividing the eight lobes into a grid. The eight lobes, by the order's reading, stand for the eightfold path; the three bands, for the threefold knowledge.

What is not standard is the engraving. Around the bulb, in the eight long vertical valleys between the ridges, climb eight thin spirals of fine-cut syllables — the seed-mantras of his body-empowerment recital, one per valley, eight in total, beginning at the lower band and running to the upper. He carved them himself with a stylus over the course of one winter, working at lamplight in his cell, one valley per fortnight. The eight valleys, by his own private reading, also stand for the eight things he has chosen not to say since his vow began.

The whole weapon weighs more than a young soldier can lift to shoulder. He carries it across his back on a hardened harness as easily as another man carries a satchel; he can also carry it grounded one-handed, butt-on-floor, the way a senior priest carries a temple-staff, when crossing a courtyard.

Personality

Calm, deliberate, deeply observant. Listens far more than he speaks — by vow, but also by inclination. The two daily speech-windows are not casual: at sunrise he tends to give short clear instructions or brief observations; at sunset he tends to ask questions, often surprising ones. The two windows have, over the years, made him an oddly excellent teacher. Junior soldiers under his command remember his sentences for years afterward.

Has an extremely dry sense of humour that surfaces almost entirely on the wax-board, in single-line written quips that the recipient will find later in the day. The party's archivist character will, by mid-campaign, have started keeping a small collection of his wax-board lines.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Heavy front-line bruiser with mantra-amplified strikes. Lohajihva is a peak Maharathi-tier mace specialist. His normal strikes are extremely heavy; his Tier B body-channelled astras add temporary buffs to specific swings — one round, the next strike pierces armour; the round after, it ignores positioning and becomes an unblockable; the round after, it briefly knocks down an entire rank of three enemies in a line.

He cycles the body-mantras during combat by silent recitation, which is permitted under his vow because no sound leaves his mouth. Players see the mantra cycle as a small visible counter on his unit card; pressing the corresponding action key swaps the next strike's effect. Most players learn within three battles to time the cycle around enemy formations.

Lore Fragment

The senior teachers of the Vāgmuni Order argued for ten years over whether their celebrated apostate had improved their lineage by leaving it or merely embarrassed it by leaving it improved. They settled the argument the way the order settles most arguments — by a vote — and the vote was inconclusive, and so the matter has been kept under review since. The current under-review is in its fourteenth year.— from the Vāgmuni Order's standing-question registry, current entry

XX
Seed · 20 — The Pit-Forged

Pāṣāṇa, The Captain Who Will Not Fight For Free

A freed pit-fighter who carries a mace forged from his own former chains, and who will not lift it for any cause that has not paid him first — even his own life.

⤿ Gadayudha 01Liminal Birth 02Atirathi · T4 03Mace 04Foot 05Astra · Tier A 06Algorithmic Vow (Payment-Bound) 07Pure Mortal 08War-Hardened 09Adharma-Exploiter
Concept

Pāṣāṇa was sold into a kingdom's blood-pits at age nine, by a famine-pressed family in a country he can no longer find on a map. He fought there for fifteen years — exhibition matches, criminal-disposal matches, the occasional inter-kingdom blood-tournament. He was, by the end of those fifteen years, the pit's senior champion, with a name on the board and a private cell with a door that locked from the inside, which is the closest thing to dignity a pit ever offered. He had also killed eighty-seven men, of whom he could name fewer than half.

At twenty-four, a freedman patron — a retired civic librarian, of all unlikely rescuers — bought him out. The patron's reasons were complicated and not entirely about Pāṣāṇa; the practical fact was that, on a winter morning, Pāṣāṇa walked out of the pit with a free man's papers and no idea what to do with his hands. The librarian took him in, fed him, gave him a back room and a stack of books, and said only: read whatever you find interesting, and tell me, in six months, what you would do if no one paid you to do anything.

Six months later Pāṣāṇa replied that he would still fight, because it was the only thing he was good at, but that he would only ever fight again for an agreed price. Not because he loved money — he didn't — but because every fight he had won in the pits had been "free" in the sense that he had not been asked, and he wanted, going forward, every swing of his mace to be a transaction he had agreed to. The librarian had nodded and drafted him a standard mercenary contract template that Pāṣāṇa has used, with small revisions, for the twenty years since.

The mace itself is the visual seal of the choice. Shortly after his manumission Pāṣāṇa took the iron of his old slave-collar — the smiths in the pits had welded it on at age nine, his civic record had been forged into it, his pit-name engraved across the band — and had it re-forged into the head of a war-mace. He carries it. He has named it nothing; he says it does not need a name. He kept the slave-name engraving on the inside surface of the mace-head, where only he can see it, where he can put his hand and feel it.

Appearance

Mid-forties; immense — six feet seven, broad through the shoulders and chest, thick through the upper arms in the way that men who lifted heavy weights for fifteen years before they were grown thicken permanently. Skin a deep mahogany-brown, scarred extensively: a long pale line from the right temple down to the jaw (taken at fourteen, second exhibition match), a constellation of small marks across both forearms (training wear), a single carefully-made scar across the left collarbone (sacramental, made by his own hand on the morning he walked out of the pit; he says nothing more about it). The body of a working man rather than a posed one — none of his scars are pretty, all of them are honest.

Hair black and going iron-grey at the temples, kept short and practical. Beard short and squared. Eyes a dark, unhurried brown, with a faint network of lines at the corners that suggest the man laughs more often than his face's resting state implies. His face's resting state is, admittedly, alarming.

Wears practical mercenary kit: a dark wine-red leather harness over a sleeveless rust-coloured tunic, heavy bracers on both forearms, a thick belt with mace-loop and money-pouch (visible — he is not embarrassed about either), riding-trousers in dark canvas, well-broken boots. The patron-librarian's small silver pendant — a stylised open book — hangs at his throat on a leather cord. He has worn it every day for twenty years.

The gadā is the classical pit-champion's preferred form, scaled to Pāṣāṇa — and Pāṣāṇa is six feet seven. Stood butt-down, the crown rises a full hand above his head; on most other men the same weapon would loom two hands taller. The haft is a single thick column of black-stained ironwood — wide enough at the grip to require both hands and a half — banded at four points in heavy dark iron, with a plain blocky iron pommel at the foot rather than a lotus (he disliked the lotus; the librarian's smith made it plain).

The head at the crown is the entire reason men step out of his lane. A single vast onion-bulb of refolded collar-iron, roughly the size of his own torso, deeply fluted into six broad vertical lobes of unequal width — three larger and three smaller, alternating in the deep classical onion-profile. The asymmetry was forced by the limited material the smith had to work with (the iron of one slave-collar will only go so far); Pāṣāṇa retained the irregularity rather than corrected it, and likes that the asymmetry shows. Two heavy raised iron bands circle the bulb horizontally — at the lower third and at the upper third — both undecorated. A flared iron collar wraps the seam where bulb meets haft. The bulb's surface is otherwise plain. No lotus cap, no finial. The librarian's smith made nothing on this weapon ornamental that Pāṣāṇa did not specifically request.

The lower band carries one stamped mark, half a coin wide: the librarian's house-sigil, a stylised open book. The pit-name engraving is hidden on the inside surface of the bulb, under the haft-fitting where the iron seats into the wood; visible only when the bulb is removed, which Pāṣāṇa has never done. The whole weapon is one of the heaviest in the army by some measurable margin — the bulb alone weighs as much as a half-grown calf. He swings it two-handed, slowly, with great economy, and the men who have stood in front of one of his strikes describe the experience, afterwards, as "like being hit by a temple bell." Most pit-fighters of his old class could not have swung this gadā at all. The point is that he can.

Personality

Blunt. Dryly funny. Surprisingly well-read — fifteen years of working through the librarian's collection during off-hours have left him with an unexpected facility for mid-period historical commentary, which he enjoys deploying in inappropriate company. Reads in his tent at night the way the young prince Nirjhara does, and would, if they ever met, have a very specific argument with him about three commentators in particular.

Surface-rough, deeply principled underneath. The "adharma-exploiter" classification is technical: he charges his employers, including allies, for every mission, including ones where the alliance is supposedly reciprocal. The kingdoms find this difficult; he does not change his terms. Underneath the contract is a man who walks a long road every winter to leave a small bag of food at the door of a hospice he will not name, and who has paid the rent on his patron-librarian's old apartment for the eight years since the librarian died, and who does not mention any of this.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Heavy bruiser; payment-gated deployment. Pāṣāṇa is one of the heaviest single-target damage-dealers in the army, particularly devastating against larger enemies — his vow gives him a +30% critical chance against any unit larger than a horse (war-elephants, mounted commanders, ogres if they exist in this setting). He does not, however, deploy without payment.

The mechanic: each chapter, the player must pay him a small gold cost from the army's treasury before deployment — the price scales with the chapter's difficulty. Payment skipped, deployment refused; he simply will not show up. Players who try to game this find that he is unbribable on both ends — neither over- nor under-payment changes his terms. He charges a fair price, takes it, and shows up, and works, exactly as agreed.

His Tier A astras are minimal — a single elemental impact-mantra he learned from his patron, used at the moment of strike. The character carries no mantric ambitions.

Signature Ability

The Bill Comes Due

Ṛṇāgraha

Pāṣāṇa names a single high-value enemy unit on the field as the "principal" of this fight — typically the enemy commander. For the rest of the battle, every wound he takes from any other source is mechanically billed to the principal: when Pāṣāṇa eventually closes with the named target, his accumulated damage-debt is added to that single strike. A long enough fight produces a single annihilating blow.

Costonce per battle; if the named principal flees the field or is killed by anyone other than Pāṣāṇa, the accumulated debt is wasted, and Pāṣāṇa loses 1 morale-stat for the rest of the chapter (a contract he could not collect on)

Gameplay Feel

"He charges. He shows up. He breaks the man you pointed him at. The numbers are not small. The man, afterwards, walks back to camp, washes the mace, and counts the contract."

Lore Fragment

The librarian's last entry in his personal accounts, dated three days before his death, reads: P. brought groceries today and refused payment. Argued with him for half an hour about it. He said he wasn't being paid for it, so it didn't violate the rule. I told him this was the wrong way round. He said the rule was the rule. We had tea. He left. The boy I bought home twenty years ago has become, by his own slightly absurd determination, a kind of philosopher. I am extremely proud of him. I have not, of course, told him so. The rule is the rule.— private journal of Achyutadāsa the Librarian, final entry
