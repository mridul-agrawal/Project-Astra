Project Astra · Character Design Reference · Vol. 02

Five Character Seeds

Sample warriors built by intersecting branches across the nine taxonomy trees. Each is a starting point — fully sketched but deliberately rough at the edges, so you can pull them apart and reshape them.

How these were assembled

Every character below is one branch from each of the nine trees in Vol. 01. The axis-strip at the top of each card lists those nine choices. The rest of the sheet — name, lore, appearance, abilities — is what fell out of those nine intersections when held in tension with each other.

Read these as first sketches, not finished concepts. The names are placeholders; the silhouettes are propositions; the stats are rough. Pick one, riff on it, redraw it. Or take three and Frankenstein them.

I
Seed · 01 — The Bound Commander

Vrishaketu, The Last Pillar of Anuhasta

An old general bound by an oath that outlived the kingdom that asked for it.

01Royalty 02Maharathi · T5 03Spear / Shakti 04Chariot 05Astra · Tier C 06Institutional Vow 07Pure Mortal 08War-Hardened 09Tragic Loyalist
Concept

The fading commander of a kingdom that no longer exists in any meaningful sense. Vrishaketu swore his service to a throne three regents ago — to a court that has since become a puppet stage for a tyrant the player faces in Act II. He knows the cause is unjust. He has known for a long time. The vow is older than his doubt, and that is the whole tragedy.

He is the kind of antagonist the player begins by hating and ends by mourning. There is a recruitment route, but it requires the death of the man he swore the oath to — a death the party did not ask for and cannot un-cause.

Appearance

Tall and gone slightly thin in the way old soldiers go thin — shoulders still wide, but the chest no longer fills the harness the way it used to. Skin the colour of weathered teak. Hair iron-grey, oiled back, bound in a single warrior's knot at the crown; the rest falls past the shoulder when loose, which is rare.

Wears a cuirass of overlapping bronze scales the colour of late-evening sun, lacquered black at the seams. A long pale shawl marked with the sigil of his vanished kingdom — three concentric rings, broken — wraps once around his waist and hangs almost to the chariot floor. His shakti, a long ceremonial spear with a leaf-shaped blade, is held in a leather sleeve at his back when not in use; the sleeve is older than the spear.

His face is the read: a long jaw, a thin mouth that rarely opens, and grey eyes set deep enough that they seem to be looking at something further away than the battlefield. A faint vertical scar runs from his hairline down through his right brow — not given in combat, but in a duel of honour decades ago that he refuses to discuss.

Personality

Quiet to the point of being mistaken for cold. Speaks in measured, complete sentences and considers it a discourtesy to interrupt anyone — including enemies. Has a dry, vanishingly subtle humour that surfaces only with men he has fought beside for thirty years.

Internally, he is at war with himself in every scene he appears in. He will not say so. The closest he comes is a habit, in conversation, of trailing off and looking at his hands.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Anchor & Denial. Vrishaketu is built to hold ground, not to chase. Once his chariot is positioned, he becomes a stationary fortress: long reach with the shakti, a Tier-C astra in reserve, and an aura that gives nearby enemy units a small morale and accuracy bonus simply by his being there. The player wants to flank around him, not engage him head-on.

His charioteer is named and statted — losing the chariot is a separate, devastating event from killing him personally. If the chariot wheel is stuck (a status condition the player can inflict), he loses his "anchor" buff entirely and his vow-bound benefits begin to falter.

Signature Ability

The Three Broken Rings

Vajra-stambha

Vrishaketu plants the butt of his shakti and recites the oath he swore to the founding king of Anuhasta. For three turns, he cannot move — and cannot be moved. His Defense and Resilience double; allies within two tiles gain his vow-bonus; enemies adjacent to him lose half their action economy.

Costlocked-in for 3 turns; if the chariot is destroyed during the stance, the vow snaps and he loses Dharma permanently for the rest of the campaign

Gameplay Feel

"You don't fight Vrishaketu. You fight the ground he is standing on, and try to outlast it."

Lore Fragment

It is said that when Anuhasta fell, three of its sons broke their oaths and lived; one kept his and burned with the city. There is a fourth, but the tellers do not agree on whether he keeps his oath or has forgotten what it was for.— from a wayside teller's verse, district unknown

II
Seed · 02 — The Cinder-Born Hunter

Indravati, Daughter of the Smokeless Flame

A girl summoned from the sacrificial fire to kill one specific man, and now uncertain what to do with the rest of her life.

01Liminal Birth 02Atirathi · T4 03Bow Specialist 04Foot 05Astra · Tier B 06Vengeance Vow 07Fire-Born 08Divine-Direct 09Corrective Vengeance
Concept

Indravati was not born; she arrived. A grieving sage's wife performed a forbidden rite over a fire of khadira wood, and the figure that stepped out had a bow already strung and a name on her lips — the name of the man who had wronged the family.

That was her purpose. Her entire identity. The catch is that the player meets her after she has fulfilled it — found him, killed him, walked back from the body — and discovered, to her own bewilderment, that she is still here. The fire did not reclaim her. She is a weapon that has been put down and is waiting to be picked up again, by anyone, for anything. The Pandavas' war finds her in this lull.

Appearance

Mid-twenties in apparent age — but she has only existed for about three years, total. Compact, wiry, slightly shorter than average; the build of a hunter, not a duellist. Skin a deep coppery brown that seems to hold heat — in low light, the inside of her wrists and the hollow of her throat have a faint warm glow that vanishes if you look directly. She does not perspire, ever.

Hair black and very long, almost always braided tight against her back in a single rope; the tip of the braid is, unmistakably, charred. Eyes the dark amber of old honey, with a fine grain of red in the iris that catches when she draws.

Her bow is plain — a forester's recurve of horn and yew, no ornament — but the arrows are unusual: black-shafted, fletched with the feathers of birds that should not be local, and warm to the touch when drawn from the quiver. She wears a sleeveless tunic of charcoal and rust, leather hunting trousers, and a single thin gold chain at her throat that no one ever sees her remove. She walks barefoot in dry weather. The grass behind her, in still air, sometimes seems to lean toward where she has just been.

Personality

Direct in a way that unsettles people. She has not been alive long enough to learn small talk, and she is not particularly interested in learning it now. Asks blunt questions; gives blunt answers; does not soften refusals.

Beneath this — and only her closest support unit ever sees it — is a quiet, almost childlike curiosity about being a person rather than a verb. She watches people eat. She watches children play. She does both with the same level attention she gives to a target at three hundred paces.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Long-range executioner. Indravati is the player's answer to high-value targets behind enemy lines. Foot-mobile and lightly armoured, she lives or dies by initiative — she wants to fire before the enemy archer-line draws breath, and reposition before they draw a second.

Her Tier-B astras are illusion- and stupefaction-based: she can darken the field around her own position for a turn, or pull a single enemy's gaze (and attack priority) off her allies and onto an empty patch of ground. She is, fundamentally, a positioner with a killshot attached.

III
Seed · 03 — The Hollow-Vow Ascetic

Bhargava-Aja, The Walker Who Will Not Sit

A young scholar-warrior who swore to neither sit nor sleep until a certain mountain falls — and the mountain still stands.

01Brahmin-Warrior 02Atirathi · T4 03Astra-Specialist 04Foot 05Astra · Tier C 06Ascetic Vow 07Pure Mortal 08Avatar-Lineage 09Dharma-Bound
Concept

Bhargava-Aja is the youngest student of a martial lineage said to descend, however dilutely, from a divine incarnation. He is brilliant, slightly insufferable about it, and as a student-graduation rite he took a vow that his teachers warned him was too ambitious: he would neither sit nor sleep until the Mountain of Sahyadri-Above — a peak said to harbour a fragment of an old, sleeping wrong — was unmade.

That was four years ago. The mountain is still there. He has not sat down since. The vow has begun to do things to him: he is faster, sharper, his mantras land harder than his teachers' did at his age — but his hands shake when he is not concentrating, and his hair has gone the white of a man twice his years.

Appearance

Twenty-six in years, sixty in the eyes. Tall and gaunt — the gauntness of a man whose body has been spending its principal, not its interest, for some time. Skin the warm olive-brown of the southern hill country, deeply tanned at the throat, paler at the wrists where the prayer-beads cover.

Hair completely white, kept short — almost cropped — because he no longer has the patience for ritual hair-binding. A close-trimmed beard, also white. Eyes a pale grey-green, the colour startling against the dark skin; rimmed in red from sleeplessness no matter how recently he has rested standing.

Dressed plainly: an undyed cotton wrap from waist to mid-calf, a longer ochre upper-cloth thrown over one shoulder, both stained at the hems. A thin sacred thread — the yajñopavīta — runs from his left shoulder to right hip, the only ornament he wears. Carries no shield; his weapon is recitation, and his hand is empty most of the time. When forced to fight at distance, he carries a slim ash-wood staff that doubles as a walking-stick on the move.

He walks constantly. Even in conversation, he paces. The few times the player sees him stop and stand still, something is about to go very wrong.

Personality

Sharp, impatient, and trying very hard not to be — he is intellectually aware that his vow has eroded his temper, and he is performing patience the way a tired actor performs a familiar role. Ferociously intelligent, often funny in a black, exhausted way. Holds himself to standards no one around him meets, and is quietly disappointed about it most of the time.

The player learns, late, that he is terrified of finishing his vow. As long as the mountain stands, he has a purpose. He has not asked himself who he will be after.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Glass nuke / mantric artillery. Bhargava-Aja is the answer to the question "what if a unit could fire a Brahmastra-tier astra and was also made of paper?" He has Tier-C astra access — uncommonly high for an Atirathi — and his recital times are short because of his lineage's mantric fluency.

The catch: his vow forbids sitting. He cannot enter cover. He cannot occupy a defensive emplacement. He is always exposed, always upright, and his Defense stat reflects it. The player who plans well places him on a high ridge with two shields between him and the enemy archer-line. The player who plans poorly loses him on turn three.

IV
Seed · 04 — The Wheelsmith's Daughter

Sumitrā, The One the Wheel Will Not Catch

A charioteer-caste girl who out-fights the warriors she is meant to drive, and a small, dangerous voice in her head says she always could.

01Liminal Birth 02Rathi · T3 03Sword (Twin) 04Horse 05Astra · Tier A 06Curse of Forgetting 07Demigod 08Self-Taught 09Adharma-Exploiter
Concept

Sumitrā's father was the master wheelwright of a minor kingdom; her mother, by the family's quiet, never-spoken account, was not. The wind itself, on a long road, in a year the rains forgot — that is the village's version. Whatever the truth, Sumitrā can run a horse three times the distance other riders manage, and she taught herself the twin-sword forms by watching warriors at sparring practice through a fence.

The establishment will not formally recognise her; she is the wrong caste for the throne to claim, and too dangerous for them to ignore. So she rides as a raid commander for a small mercenary band — striking supply columns, escorting refugees, taking grey jobs and grey money. She is also, quietly, cursed: a sage she cheated at dice — and she did cheat — left her with a single, surgical wound. At the moment her astras are about to hit, she forgets the recall mantra. Her elemental shots therefore tend to keep going long after they should stop.

Appearance

Twenty-two, average height, the broad-shouldered build of someone who has been working with hands and horses since childhood. Skin the warm bronze of long sun, with a fine network of small scars along the forearms — not battle wounds, working wounds. The kind a wheelwright's apprentice gets and never quite outgrows.

Hair the colour of dark honey at the roots and almost grey-blonde at the ends — not natural, almost certainly the inheritance of a divine wind-line on her mother's side. Cut to chin length, uneven, hacked back with her own knife when it gets in her eyes. Eyes a pale steel-grey, with one tiny imperfection: a single drop of darker pigment in the right iris, like an inkdrop on water.

Wears a short-sleeved leather riding-jerkin over a plum-coloured tunic, the leather dyed twilight-violet because she likes the colour and there is no one in her band who will tell her not to. Loose riding trousers, tall boots, a long indigo riding-shawl wound twice around the throat against road-dust. Twin short swords at the hip, drawn cross-handed; a small bow on the saddle for distance work she rarely uses. Her horse is named — and the horse, frankly, is half her character: a tall grey gelding she stole back from people who had stolen him from her.

Personality

Quick-tongued, charming, profoundly untrustworthy with money but utterly trustworthy with people. The kind who will lie to a magistrate's face and ride a hundred miles to bury a stranger's brother because he asked. Laughs easily; gets quiet when she is genuinely angry, which is rare and frightening.

Has an ongoing, almost theological argument with the idea of dharma. Will steal a Brahmin's grain to feed a village and feel righteous about it. Knows that this is, by some accountings, the wrong answer. Does not care. The curse has, if anything, hardened her position: fine, the universe is keeping score. Send the bill.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Mounted skirmisher / flank duellist. Sumitrā is what the player reaches for when there is a high-value enemy archer or mage three squares behind the front line and someone needs to be there yesterday. High movement, very high crit, twin-sword form gives her two strikes per engagement; she does not tank, she visits.

Her Tier-A astras (wind, lightning) are usable but unreliable because of the curse — there is a small percentage chance, increasing as her HP drops, that the astra cannot be recalled and continues to damage a random adjacent unit (friend or enemy) for one more turn. The player learns to use them only when collateral damage is acceptable.

V
Seed · 05 — The Silver-Thumb

Kalyāṇ-Ekajit, The One Who Paid In Advance

A forester who learned the bow from a clay idol of a master who refused to teach him, and whose right thumb is now made of silver.

01Liminal Birth 02Maharathi · T5 03Bow Specialist 04Foot 05Astra · Tier B 06Apprenticeship · Guru-Dakshina 07Pure Mortal 08Self-Taught + Idol 09Strategic-Neutral
Concept

Kalyāṇ was a forester's son in a tribe the mainline kingdoms politely call vana-jana — forest people — and impolitely call several other things. He learned of a great archery master, walked to the nearest court to ask for training, and was refused on grounds of caste so flatly that no one even argued the point.

So he went home, made a small clay figure of the master, set it under a banyan, and taught himself by treating the idol as his teacher. Years passed. He became, by any honest measure, the best archer in the region, possibly in the kingdom. Then the master heard. Came in person. Demanded the traditional fee — the guru-dakshina — and asked for the boy's right thumb. Kalyāṇ gave it.

He should have been crippled. He was, briefly. Then a wandering smith heard the story, made him a thumb of silver, and fitted it to the stump in a process Kalyāṇ does not discuss. He shoots again. He shoots almost as well as before — almost. The "almost" is the whole story.

Appearance

Mid-thirties. Average build, deceptively unimposing — strangers consistently underestimate him on first meeting. Skin the deep umber-brown of long, hard sun; the carriage of a man used to walking thirty miles a day with a bow over his shoulder. Hair black and threaded with the first silver, kept long, tied at the nape; a short, neat beard.

Eyes very dark, very steady — the eyes of a man whose default state is aim. Few expressions; when he smiles, which is rare, it is small and warm and entirely sincere.

The silver thumb on his right hand is the centrepiece of his silhouette. It is well-made — articulated at the joint by tiny river-pearl pins — but it is unmistakably not flesh, and he does not hide it: he keeps it polished, almost vain about it. He pulls his bowstring with the silver thumb. The string sings differently against silver than against bone.

He wears the deep teal hunter's cowl of his tribe — a wrap-coat of waxed cotton, knee-length, dyed with river-clay — over plain undyed trousers and soft leather wraps to the knee. A great curved bow of dark horn is slung over his back at all times; he is never seen without it. A short skinning-knife at the hip; nothing else.

Personality

Quiet, watchful, profoundly self-contained. The opposite of bitter — there are reasons he could be bitter, and he has examined each one and decided against it. Speaks rarely. When he does speak, he is often funny in a very dry way that takes a while to land.

Unaligned by choice. Will fight for either side of the war if the side has clean reasons; will not fight for either if neither does. The mainline kingdoms therefore court him constantly and resent that he cannot be bought. He is the player's negotiation puzzle in Act II — the sequence where the party realises they cannot recruit him with money, blackmail, divine providence, or appeals to caste loyalty, and must instead simply be the better cause.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Sniper / killing reservoir. Kalyāṇ is a Maharathi-tier archer — the highest tier of any character in this set — and on the field, he plays exactly that way: long-range, surgical, almost surgically uninvolved. He picks one target per turn and that target dies. He does not chase, panic, or make trades.

His silver thumb gives him a permanent −1 to attack speed compared to a flesh-thumbed archer of equal training: a small, recurring tax for the rest of his life. The player feels it in initiative order. He always fires, but he does not always fire first. This single mechanical detail tells the entire story of his character without any dialogue.
