Project Astra · Character Design Reference · Vol. 04

The Six Signature Combinations

Volume 01 listed six recurring intersections that the source material kept producing — patterns the epics return to because they hold real narrative weight. This volume gives each pattern a body. Six characters, freshly invented in every specific, each one a working answer to "what does this combination actually look like."

About these six

The six patterns from Volume 01: The Cursed Apocalypse, The Bound Commander, The Insulted Demigod, The Reluctant Annihilator, The Devoted Second, and The Held-Back Nuke. Each is a tension, not a character — a shape the universe likes to bend warriors into.

Volume 02's old general Vrishaketu was an early take on The Bound Commander — an old elephant-borne Royalty figure. To avoid duplication, the version here approaches the same combination from a wholly different silhouette: a young queen-regent / civic peace-broker, on foot, in court robes, whose vow is to a city rather than to a throne. Same tension; different body.

Across all six: no thumbs taken, no charioteer-children, no fire-births. The pattern is inherited from the trees; the textures are entirely our own.

XI
Combination · 01 — The Cursed Apocalypse

Kṣatāyu, The Inherited Mantra

A scholar who woke one morning fluent in an astra they were never taught — and is paying for the knowledge in pieces of who they used to be.

⤿ Cursed Warrior + Apocalyptic Astra 01Brahmin-Warrior 02Atirathi · T4 03Astra-Specialist 04Foot 05Astra · Tier D 06Curse of Forgetting 07Pure Mortal 08Avatar-Lineage (inherited, untaught) 09Strategic-Neutral
Concept

Kṣatāyu's mother held one of the four surviving lineage-mantras of a Tier D astra called the Tamovarṣa — the Rain of Unmaking — and she had refused, all her life, to teach it to anyone, including her own child. She kept the mantra somewhere behind her teeth and would not let it out.

She died in winter, in her sleep, of nothing in particular. The next morning Kṣatāyu woke up with the entire mantra in their head, in perfect order, in their mother's voice.

This should not be possible. The mantra is supposed to transfer only by deliberate teaching, after years of preparation, with rites and witnesses. Kṣatāyu knows it the way a person knows their own street. They can recite it in their sleep. They have, in fact, recited it in their sleep — and the room shook.

The cost reveals itself slowly. Each time Kṣatāyu performs the full recital, the astra grows more complete and more dangerous — and Kṣatāyu loses a thread of who they were. A childhood friend's face. A grandfather's name. The taste of a specific sweet. The year they learned to swim. Memory, paid out in small irreversible coins, on each use. The sage their mother once consulted explained it, when found, in a single sentence: the mantra is using your past as fuel; when there is no past left, the mantra will be complete and you will not be.

Appearance

Late twenties, slim, of average height — the unassuming build of a long-time scholar rather than a warrior. Skin a pale sandalwood-brown, fair for the region they grew up in. Dark hair, kept long and tied loosely at the nape with a length of grey cotton; a few strands have already turned white at the roots, an early flag of the curse — the recital is not only eating memory, it is taking colour from the head where the memory was kept.

Eyes a soft, slightly indistinct grey-brown, with the unfocused quality of someone who is, at any moment, not entirely sure where in their own life they are. They look at people kindly and a beat too long. Sometimes they begin to greet a friend by the wrong name and correct themselves mid-syllable. Their dialogue style accommodates this: pauses, half-finished sentences, courteous redirection.

Wears the plain undyed cottons of a wandering scholar — a long tunic to mid-thigh, loose drawstring trousers, a simple charcoal-grey overcloak in cold weather. No order's robes, no caste-marks beyond a small ash line on the forehead they apply themselves each morning. Carries no weapon. Their only kit is a small palm-leaf manuscript-bundle on a leather strap at the shoulder — and the manuscript is half blank, because they are writing into it, by hand, every night, the things they are afraid of forgetting next. A list of names. A description of a kitchen. The way their mother used to laugh. The book is the back-up of their own self.

The most uncanny detail: in any room with a still water-vessel — a temple basin, a courtyard cistern — the surface of the water trembles slightly when Kṣatāyu walks past. Not their footsteps. Something inside them.

Personality

Quiet, mild, observant. Treats every conversation as potentially the last conversation they will remember having with this person, and accordingly listens with a care other characters find moving. They were, before all this, a textual scholar — a translator of old battlefield commentaries, professionally inclined toward precision — and that habit survives. They will pause to make sure they have understood you correctly. They will write down your name in their book if they think it's at risk.

Strategically neutral because they have run out of bandwidth for partisanship. Will fight for whichever side will keep them fed and unrobbed long enough to finish writing what is left of their childhood into the book.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Apocalyptic mantric artillery on a degrading clock. Kṣatāyu can deploy the Tamovarṣa partial form on most missions — a heavy 3×3 area damage that does not yet wipe a battlefield clean. Each use of the partial form costs a memory; the player sees this in the UI as a small checklist item permanently greyed out (a friend's name, a favourite food, a year). The full form is far more powerful but costs the campaign's worth of memories at once.

Mechanically: each partial-form use also raises a counter. At 7 uses, the next deployment is the full Tamovarṣa, and Kṣatāyu becomes an unrecoverable NPC the morning after — alive but no longer the person the player knew. The player decides, across the campaign, how many partials to spend and whether to ever pull the trigger on the full form. Most players will not.

Lore Fragment

There is a journal in the temple at Ṣaḍāntara, kept under glass, written in a single small hand. It begins with a list of names — three hundred and forty-one of them, in the first nine pages alone — and continues, for the rest of the volume, in increasingly careful descriptions of objects: a brass cup, a window, a particular bend of a particular river. The author signed only the first page. The signature is illegible; it has been read aloud over the journal a thousand times since, and the author does not, any longer, look up.— from a temple registrar's catalogue, Ṣaḍāntara

XII
Combination · 02 — The Bound Commander

Anuhitā, Vow-Wife of Karavīra

A young queen-regent whose ritual oath was sworn to her city's continuance, not its monarchy — and the city's king has just gone mad.

⤿ Dharma-Bound Commander + Institutional Vow 01Royalty 02Maharathi · T5 03Bow + Sword (Hybrid) 04Foot 05Astra · Tier C 06Institutional Vow 07Pure Mortal 08Institutional School 09Dharma-Bound
Concept

The Bound Commander is the warrior who fights for the wrong side because they cannot break their vow. Volume 02's Vrishaketu is one shape of this — an old general resigned to it. Anuhitā is the inverse: young, active, still arguing. The vow has not finished settling on her shoulders yet, and the player meets her in the middle of the negotiation she is having with herself.

At her coronation as queen-regent of the city of Karavīra — twelve years ago, when she was nineteen — she took the high vow of Pura-Vivāha ("the marriage to a city"): a sacred bond not to the throne, not to the dynasty, but to the city's continuance itself. While she lives, Karavīra will not fall. That was the contract. She made it freely. The city, in turn, accepted her — by ritual, by record, by the assent of the seven guild-masters and the river.

Twelve years on, the man on Karavīra's throne — her uncle by half-blood, regent before her — has gone, in the careful phrasing of the court physicians, away from his own counsel. He is starting wars Karavīra cannot win. He is enacting decrees the citizenry cannot obey. He is, in any real sense, the most dangerous threat to Karavīra's continuance currently breathing — and Anuhitā cannot move against him, because the city's continuance and the city's monarchy are, by every official register, the same legal entity. She has read the founding charters herself. She has read them three times. The argument does not get easier.

So she defends him. Loudly, in public; quietly, in private, with her hands shaking. She rides at the head of his armies. She wins his battles. She has begun, in the last six months, to lose weight she did not have to spare.

Appearance

Early thirties. Of medium height, slim through the shoulders, the runner's build of a woman who took her training seriously and does not let it lapse. Skin a warm caramel-brown, slightly drawn at the cheeks now from worry. Hair the colour of dark wet wood, kept long and worn in a single low braid down the back, threaded twice with thin gold cord — a queen-regent's mark of office; she has never taken it out, even sleeping.

Eyes a clear cinnamon-brown, deeply set, very still. The face of a person who has been asked difficult questions in council chambers since she was twenty and has long ago stopped giving anything away in public. In private — once or twice in the campaign — the player will catch her with the public face off, and what's underneath is exhausted.

Wears the formal armour of Karavīra's court: an articulated cuirass of dark bronze inlaid with the city's seven-petaled lotus in pale ivory, over a long tunic of antique brass-gold silk that falls to mid-calf, slit at the sides for riding. A long civic stole of deep saffron crosses her chest from left shoulder to right hip — the stole is the visible sign of the Pura-Vivāha bond, and only a bound queen-regent of Karavīra has the right to wear it. She has not removed it in twelve years; it is repaired, not replaced. The hem is fringed with a single line of city-script: asti yāvat asmi yāvat — "while I am, the city is."

Her weapons: a slim recurve bow of dark horn, decorated only with a single carved lotus at the grip, and a short straight-bladed sword at the right hip — a court-issue pattern. She is genuinely a hybrid combatant; both arms are real, both are practiced. Her chariot-driver is her older sister-cousin, who has not spoken to her about the king in eight months, and the silence between them is the loudest thing in any room they share.

Personality

Composed in public, fierce in negotiation, quietly tortured in private. Speaks beautifully — the seven-year court-rhetoric training shows — and uses the skill ruthlessly to defend an indefensible position. Has, at this point in her career, won three high-stakes diplomatic exchanges that she privately considers the worst victories of her life. The player can, by the end of Act II, get her to admit this; she will not say it twice.

Genuinely pious in a non-doctrinal way — the vow she swore is, to her, sacred, not legal, and she will not disrespect her own past self by treating it as anything else. She is also, increasingly, looking for a textual scholar who can read the charters more cleverly than she can. The party's resident philosopher-character will, at some point, find her in their tent at midnight, with the founding documents spread on the bedroll, asking very specific, very desperate questions.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Field commander & morale anchor. Anuhitā is a Maharathi-tier hybrid bow-and-blade — long-range opener, mid-range follow-up, both very accurate. Her Tier C astra is restrained: an elemental ward that protects allies in a small radius rather than damaging enemies. She is built to preserve. Her aura grants nearby allies +1 to Defense and +1 to morale-saves while she is on the field.

The mechanical cost of her vow: any unit she has personally killed cannot be looted by the player's faction, ever. Their bodies are returned, their weapons are returned, their possessions are returned — by Karavīran civic law, which she enforces personally. She will end her own turn early to bury an enemy. The player adjusts their economy around this. Some find it inconvenient. Some find it the most important thing about her.

XIII
Combination · 03 — The Insulted Demigod

Tantrīka, The Bow That Was A String

An itinerant musician of the road-shrine god, whose stringed instrument becomes a literal bow when the music turns from wedding to war.

⤿ Low-Born + Divinely Gifted 01Liminal Birth 02Atirathi · T4 03Bow / Unique (Veena-Bow) 04Foot 05Astra · Tier B 06(no formal vow — see notes) 07Demigod (Crossroads-Line) 08Self-Taught 09Strategic-Neutral
Concept

The Insulted Demigod, in the source material, is the warrior of impeccable divine lineage who is denied recognition because of where they were raised — and whose resentment is the fuel of their power. Tantrīka inverts the resentment cleanly: he is divinely lineaged, he is denied recognition, he is insulted regularly — and he does not particularly care. He has work. He has a road. He has people who like the music. The mainline kingdoms can keep their court rolls.

Tantrīka was raised by an itinerant musician-troupe — the kind that travels between villages performing at weddings, harvest-festivals, funerals, anything that pays — and his lineage is on no temple's official register because the deity it leads back to is on no temple's official register. His father, by every honest account, is the god of the road-shrine: a minor deity of the small wayside altars where travellers leave a flower and a coin and a prayer for safe passage. The road-shrine god is a real god. He simply has no priesthood, no festival, and no presence in the high temples. He is, as it were, low-born among gods.

The lineage manifests in Tantrīka's instrument. He plays the veena — a long stringed lap-instrument with a resonating gourd at one end. When he plays for a wedding, it is a string-instrument. When he plays for a war, the same instrument becomes a literal bow: the long arched neck of the veena is the bow's grip, the bridge becomes a nocking-point, and the music's rhythm and tension transfer into the draw-weight. The transformation is fluid and largely visual; he does not switch weapons, he switches tunings.

Appearance

Mid-thirties, of medium height and lean build — the body of a man who walks ten miles a day with a heavy instrument over his back. Skin a deep terracotta-brown, sun-darkened almost everywhere it is exposed. Hair black, kept loose and shoulder-length, often slightly road-dusty; he is in the habit of running a hand through it before performances and looking, briefly, like a man who has not just walked thirty miles to be there. A short black moustache, no beard.

Eyes a warm dark-brown, with — and this is the only physical sign of his lineage — a small, very dim flicker of gold deep in the pupil that surfaces only when he plays the instrument under stress. In a quiet hall, no one sees it. On a battlefield, by lamplight, with the wrong note in the air, his eyes briefly look like the road-shrine lamps lit at dusk.

Wears the road-troupe's mismatched, cheerful clothes: a wrap-tunic in faded terracotta and dust-yellow, a long sash in a different shade of orange, loose road-trousers tied at the ankle, a thin cotton shawl over one shoulder against the sun. A row of small brass coin-pendants around his neck — these are travellers' offerings he has been given, over the years, by people grateful for music; he does not melt them down or sell them. He wears them. He says they sound when he walks. They do.

The veena-bow is the centrepiece of his silhouette: a long, gently arched instrument of seasoned dark teak, with a rounded gourd-resonator at one end inlaid with mother-of-pearl in a pattern of small road-shrines. Strings of finely twisted gut. When unplayed, it hangs at his back on a leather strap. When played-as-bow, the gourd serves as a counterweight at the lower limb, and the music — actual music, audible — sounds quietly while the arrows fly. The arrows themselves are hand-cut hardwood, fletched with the feathers of tame courier-pigeons from a friend's loft. Nothing about the kit looks like a warrior's kit. Almost everything in it kills.

Personality

Cheerful. Generous. Slightly gossipy. Knows everyone on every road within four kingdoms and remembers their children's names. Has, in his life, been refused entry to seven royal courts on caste grounds; has, over the same period, been welcomed to the marketplaces of all seven, and prefers the marketplaces. The mainline kingdoms have, slowly, realised that the man who knows every village headman in the region is professionally useful, and a few of them have tried, awkwardly, to formalise him. He has politely declined each time. He likes his work.

The "insult" of his combination is constant and ongoing — and he treats it exactly the way a man with somewhere better to be treats a nuisance. He is, fundamentally, not about the insult. The party finds this restful in a way they did not expect.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Long-range support archer with rhythm-based mechanics. Tantrīka's accuracy and damage are tied to a small visible raga-meter in the UI. Each turn he plays, the meter advances by one stage of the raga he chose pre-mission (slow opening → development → climax). At opening stage he does modest damage; at climax he is one of the heaviest single-shot attackers in the army. If he is interrupted mid-turn (struck, pushed, silenced), the raga resets — players who learn this protect him with two front-liners.

His Tier B astras are sound-based: a song that briefly stuns enemies in a 3-tile cone (the Suraprapāta, "fall-of-tones"), a counter-song that breaks enemy stuns. He cannot be deployed in any mission with a silence tile-flag — temple sieges where speech is forbidden, ambushes underwater, etc.

XIV
Combination · 04 — The Reluctant Annihilator

Rohiṇikā, The Last Voice of the Moon-Order

Senior priestess of an order whose Tier D astra has not been spoken in eight generations — and the eight predecessors who spoke it left only meditations on what it cost them.

⤿ Brahmin-Warrior + Astra-Specialist (Reluctant) 01Priestly-Born Warrior 02Maharathi · T5 03Astra-Specialist 04Foot 05Astra · Tier D 06Institutional Vow (Restraint) 07Pure Mortal 08Avatar-Lineage (Lunar) 09Dharma-Bound
Concept

The Reluctant Annihilator is a warrior who possesses a world-ending capability and is, by knowledge of what that capability does, the warrior most reluctant to deploy it. The reluctance is not cowardice. It is documented institutional memory. They know exactly what it costs.

Rohiṇikā is the senior living priestess — and now, after the last winter's plague, the only living priestess of full rank — of the Candragṛha Order, a small lunar-ascetic lineage that lives in a hill-temple north of Aśmavāra. The Order is the last living lineage of a Tier D astra called the Pūrṇimā-Hāra ("Full-Moon Theft") — a recital that, in deep summary, draws down the moon's silver light into a single column of pure inverse-radiance, capable of clearing a battlefield by extracting all motion from it. Every living thing in the column simply stops.

The astra has not been spoken in eight generations. Rohiṇikā knows the mantra. She has rehearsed it under her breath, alone in the temple's high gallery, on full moons; she has never spoken it aloud. By the Order's records, the last eight priestesses to perform the recital each finished a private meditation and then took their own life within the same lunar cycle. The Order keeps these meditations in a small lacquer box in the gallery. There are eight folded sheets in the box. Rohiṇikā has read all of them. She has copied them out, one per year, in her own hand, every year for the past nine years. She knows each predecessor by their handwriting now.

The campaign's central problem, for her, is whether this is the year she will need to add the ninth folded sheet to the box.

Appearance

Late forties. Of average height and slim build — her order's tradition is moderate fasting, and she has kept it. Skin a fair, almost moonstone pallor — the Candragṛha priestesses live high, in cold air, mostly indoors among scrolls — with very fine creases at the corners of the eyes from a lifetime of close reading. Hair fully grey, not white — silver-grey, the colour of old brushed bronze — kept long and braided into a single thick rope down the back, pinned at the base with a small clasp shaped like a half-moon. No fringe; the forehead is fully bare and marked, every morning, with a small horizontal line of grey ash.

Eyes a clear pale-grey — the colour of moonlight on still water. Direct, searching, kind. She has, over decades of pastoral counselling within the Order, developed the habit of really looking at the person she is speaking to, and this still discomfits people who meet her for the first time.

Wears the Order's robes: a long ash-grey under-tunic to mid-calf, a paler silver-grey upper-cloth wrapped twice around the body and thrown over the left shoulder, a wide pale moonstone-blue stole that drops behind her in cold weather. A simple hemp cord around the waist. No jewellery beyond the half-moon clasp at the braid. She wears a string of small unpolished moonstone prayer-beads at her left wrist — the beads are dull, not luminous, and she runs them between her fingers when working through a difficult clause in a text.

Her most distinctive piece of kit is the lacquer box she carries with her at all times — black-lacquered wood, the size of a forearm, with a small silver hinge and no clasp. Inside are the eight predecessor-meditations and a ninth folded sheet, blank, signed only with her own name in the upper left corner. She carries the box as casually as another character carries a knife. The party will figure out what it is sometime in Act II, and the realisation lands hard.

Personality

Calm. Gentle. Profoundly literate. Speaks in clear, considered sentences and listens with a quality of attention most characters in the army have not encountered before. Has a small dry sense of humour that surfaces almost exclusively in conversations about death, which she has thought about more than anyone the player will meet.

Her institutional vow is one of restraint — the order's senior living priestess does not, under any circumstance, deploy the Pūrṇimā-Hāra except in a defined ritual extremity (the "Three Conditions": no alternative remains; the targets meet a strict catalogue of cosmic-imbalance signs; the priestess herself has spent a full lunar cycle in retreat first). She has spent, by mid-campaign, two retreats. She has not yet recited.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Mantric defender — and a sealed apocalypse on a long fuse. Rohiṇikā is mechanically a Tier C-only unit for most of the campaign: she casts protective wards (lunar-light auras that grant nearby allies +2 Resilience and a small chance to nullify mantric attacks), and she plays the support role beautifully. She does not, will not, recite the Tier D.

The Tier D unlock is a multi-stage gameplay event — not a button. The player must, over several chapters, satisfy the Order's Three Conditions: (1) lose two named allies in a single chapter (alternative-exhaustion); (2) confirm three cosmic-imbalance signs through dialogue and observation; (3) keep Rohiṇikā out of all combat for one full chapter (her retreat). Only then does the recital become available — and only once. After it is used, Rohiṇikā writes the ninth sheet, places it in the box, leaves it in the temple's high gallery, and walks into the snow. The player does not see her again.

It is a slow-fuse weapon. The Order asks the player, mechanically and narratively, whether they are willing to bring her here — and if not, to leave the recital unspoken.

XV
Combination · 05 — The Devoted Second

Sahodayī, The Co-Risen Sister

Sworn-sister to a princess by a ritual older than either of them — better than her princess at three of four disciplines, never the better one in public, never resentful, and exactly where she means to be.

⤿ Tragic Loyalist + Skill Ceiling Below Leader 01Pure Warrior-Caste 02Atirathi · T4 03Multi-Weapon 04Horse 05Astra · Tier B 06Oath-Sister Vow (Sahodaya) 07Pure Mortal 08Institutional School 09Tragic Loyalist
Concept

The Devoted Second, in the source material, is a warrior who serves a greater warrior — usually a younger brother to a divine elder — and accepts the second place permanently, even where they could have surpassed it. Sahodayī takes the same shape, with the gendered vertical removed and the affection elevated. She is not a younger sister waiting in the wings. She is an oath-sister, of the same age, by ritual rather than blood — and her entire orientation is horizontal-loyal.

Sahodayī and the Princess Avantī of Sumitra-Pura were "co-risen" in the Sahodaya rite — a ceremony performed at six years old, on the same dawn, where two girls take a single oath that binds them as ritual sisters for life. The oath is unsentimental. It has technical, witnessable conditions: sleeping in the same room until the elder is married, eating from the same plate at the equinoxes, fighting in the same line, and — the one that matters here — never bearing arms against each other, ever, even hypothetically, even in jest. It has been performed in Sumitra-Pura for nine centuries; it is taken seriously.

Twenty-five years on, Avantī is the heir-presumptive and a competent young queen-in-waiting. Sahodayī is her sworn second, her bodyguard, her field commander, and — by the quiet assessment of every weapons-master who has trained both of them — better than Avantī at three of four classical disciplines. The fourth — strategic command — is the discipline Avantī is famous for, and the one that matters for inheriting a kingdom. Sahodayī has, with characteristic lack of drama, decided that being better at the three things that are not the famous one is enough, and she is right.

Appearance

Mid-twenties. Tall, broad-shouldered, the build of an active cavalry officer. Skin a deep umber-brown, sun-darkened at the forearms and the back of the neck. Hair black, straight, very thick — kept short for her, which is to say cropped at chin-length, tucked behind both ears in a no-nonsense fashion. Has a small white scar across the left eyebrow from a training mishap when she was twelve, the only time her princess has ever drawn blood from her, even by accident; both of them remember which day, which hour, and which dog was barking in the courtyard.

Eyes a steady dark-brown, open, easy-to-meet. The face of a person who has known almost everyone she loves since she was six years old, and is comfortable.

Wears the cavalry uniform of Sumitra-Pura's royal guard — a peacock-blue cuirass over a dark-grey undertunic, peacock-blue riding-skirt to the knee, riding-boots in dark leather. The peacock-blue is the princess's livery; Sahodayī's own colour, by birth, would be a pale gold, and she has never worn it once since the Sahodaya rite at age six. The princess wears peacock and gold both. Sahodayī wears only peacock. The asymmetry is not an oversight; it is, in old Sumitra-Pura tradition, the visible mark of the sworn second.

She rides a tall bay mare named Kāntā ("the Beloved"), and carries a single straight-bladed cavalry sword at the right hip and a war-pick in a saddle-loop. She also has, in a leather case at the saddle, a small bag of a dozen weapon-tokens — institutional-school habit; she has trained on most things — but she has not opened the bag in seven years. The sword and the pick have been enough. Around her left wrist, a single thin peacock-blue cord — the visible Sahodaya knot, retied every equinox by Avantī's own hands.

Personality

Warm, dry-humoured, unhurried. Notices everything; comments on almost nothing. Trusts her princess utterly and is trusted in return; their shared dialogue is one of the warmest dynamics in the army, full of small unfinished sentences, references the player will not catch, and very specific gentle teasing.

The "tragic" part of tragic loyalist is light here, on purpose. Sahodayī is not tragic in the Lakshmana sense — she does not die for her sister-figure, she does not waste her own life, she does not sublimate. She has chosen this, fully, with eyes open, knowing she could have ruled somewhere else. The tragedy, when it surfaces, is one specific scene: late in the campaign, an enemy sage will offer to dissolve the Sahodaya bond, on the grounds that her gifts are wasted in second-rank. She will refuse, calmly and quickly. The player will see, in the half-second after, what her face looks like when no one is watching. Then it goes back to easy.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Mounted multi-discipline duellist; co-deploy unit. Sahodayī is built around adjacency: when she and Avantī are within three tiles of each other on the battlefield, both gain +2 to Skill, +1 to Defense, and a passive ability called Sahodaya-Sahāya ("the help of the co-risen"), which lets either of them spend one of her own actions to grant the other a free counter-attack. They function, mechanically, as a two-body unit. Separating them is the player's most common mistake.

Solo, Sahodayī is a good mid-tier cavalry officer. Adjacent to Avantī, she is a Maharathi-equivalent. Avantī, similarly, is sharper-edged at her sister's side. The combination is more than the sum.

Lore Fragment

A wandering sage once asked the princess of Sumitra-Pura whether her sworn second was wasted in the second seat — the question being a polite cover for an offer of dissolution. The princess replied, with apparent puzzlement, that she had never met anyone in her own seat, and the sage left without a clearer answer. Some years later it occurred to the sage that the answer he received had been complete.— told by a junior court-poet to an apprentice scribe, late summer at the Sumitra-Pura riverside

Combination · 06 — The Held-Back Nuke

Aṅgāra-Hāsa, The Sage's Last Gift

A duellist who killed a sage in honest combat thirty years ago — and was given, by the dying man, a single arrow he can only loose against someone he loves.

Concept

Thirty years ago, Aṅgāra-Hāsa — a young sword-and-bow officer of one of the eastern marcher-kingdoms — fought a duel of honour against an old sage who had insulted his commander. He won. The sage was a peaceable man and had picked up the sword poorly; the duel was as honest as the rules allowed but not, in any spiritual sense, balanced. The sage, dying, did not curse him. The sage, dying, gave him a gift.

The gift was a single arrow — black-shafted, bone-fletched, of no apparent provenance — and a single condition. The arrow, the sage said, carries the weight of a Tier D astra. It will fly straight. It will pass through any defence. But it can only be loosed against a target Aṅgāra-Hāsa loves. The sage's argument, delivered in the last minute of his life, was clean: you killed me without knowing me. I leave you a weapon that requires you to know whom you kill. If you cannot bear to fire it, you cannot bear, perhaps, to take the life it would end. That seems fair to me.

Aṅgāra-Hāsa has carried the arrow for thirty years. It hangs in a black quiver — empty otherwise — on a peg above his sleeping-mat in every camp he has pitched since. He has been close to firing it twice: once when an enemy commander turned out to be his estranged younger brother; once when a friend of forty years, gone over to the other side, was wounded and asked, with great formality, to be killed. He did not, either time. He used a different arrow.

The campaign's question, which it does not answer until the final act, is: what circumstance could possibly arise that he would love an enemy enough to fire?

Appearance

Late fifties. Tall, weathered, the body of a man who has fought professionally for forty years and who is still, by mid-morning training, alarmingly fast. Skin a deep walnut-brown, lined at the corners of the eyes and across the back of the neck. Hair fully grey, kept long and tied back in a single loose tail at the nape; a short, neat grey beard. He has the posture of an officer who has, by long habit, learned to take up no more space than he needs.

Eyes a dark steady brown, with — when he laughs, which is rarer now than it used to be — small bright lines at the corners that earned him his name. Aṅgāra-Hāsa means "ember-laugh"; in his twenties he had a hard, bright, brief laugh like a shower of sparks, and the name stuck. He still has the laugh, though it surfaces less often, and when it does it tends to surprise people.

Wears a working officer's kit: a hardened leather cuirass in dark wine-brown, lined and reinforced at the shoulders, over a long dark-red undertunic to mid-thigh. A wide black sash, riding-trousers, well-broken boots. A short straight cavalry sword at the right hip; a working bow of horn-and-yew slung at his back along with a quiver of standard war-arrows. He rides a tall, calm, dark-coated stallion he has had for twelve years and refuses to discuss in any detail.

The black quiver hangs separately at the saddle — a small, narrow, lacquer-black case, plain, with a single dark-iron clasp. Inside is one arrow. He does not draw it. The other warriors in the army know the quiver is there. They do not ask.

Personality

Quiet, courteous, careful. Long-pause speaker — leaves room in conversations for the other person to think, and is not uncomfortable with the silence. Reads as cool until you serve under him for a season, at which point reads as kind; he makes a point of remembering the names of the youngest soldiers under his command, and writing letters home for them when their literacy isn't up to it.

Strategically neutral by long professional habit. Has fought for four different commanders across a thirty-year career. The thing he has not changed is the arrow. The arrow is the part of him that does not negotiate.

Privately, the gift has become a kind of meditation for him. He has, slowly, decided that the sage was right — that the arrow's condition is not a curse but a teaching, and that thirty years of carrying it has made him a better officer and a quieter man. He has not, he notes ruefully, become quieter enough to forget about it. But almost.

Tactical Role · Battlefield Behaviour

Veteran officer / mid-tier mounted hybrid combatant. In ordinary play, Aṅgāra-Hāsa is a solidly capable Maharathi-tier sword-and-bow rider. He is not the heaviest hitter in the army nor the fastest. He is reliable. He flanks competently, holds a line, fires accurately at mid-range, and never, ever, breaks. He is the unit the player learns to send to the part of the map they have not had time to plan for.

The arrow is held in reserve. Mechanically, the player has access to a "Black Quiver" command at all times — but the command's targeting reticle only activates over allied units. Pointing it at an enemy returns "the arrow does not yet fly." Players will, predictably, try to fire it at every difficult enemy in the early game; it will not loose.

The reticle activates, for the first time, only after a specific late-game story event the player did not foresee — at which point the apocalyptic shot becomes available, with full Tier D damage, ignoring all defences. The shot can only be fired once. After it is fired, Aṅgāra-Hāsa retires from the campaign. The empty black quiver is hung on a peg in the army's camp; he does not take it down again.

Lore Fragment

The dying sage's gift was, by the witnesses' agreement, the most polite curse anyone in the eastern marches had heard delivered.
